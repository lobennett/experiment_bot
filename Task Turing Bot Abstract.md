Title: Agentic AI can generalize to complete multiple tasks with human-like behavior

Tentative Author List: Logan J. Bennett, Russell A. Poldrack, & Patrick G. Bissett

**Introduction:** Behavioral data acquisition including in psychological science has traditionally been based upon participants physically coming to laboratories, often on college campuses, to complete experimental studies. This in-person strategy limited the rate of data acquisition and resulted in demographically biased samples (REF).
Over the last two decades, much of in-person behavioral data acquisition has given way to online experiments. This drastically increases the rate of data acquisition, has been shown to result in samples that are more representative of the population (REF), and can be implemented at reduced cost. These experiments are often done through online experimental marketplaces including Amazon Mechanical Turk and Prolific. Rigorous work had validated the data quality from these marketplaces (REF), but this validation work preceded the advent of LLMs and agentic AI.
There is growing concern and evidence that online experiments may be contaminated by AI-based bots. One source of data that is particularly susceptible to AI bots are self-report surveys, which involve verbal prompts and verbal responses with minimal emphasis on response speed, making them particularly susceptible to LLMs. Westwood (2025) recently showed that an LLM-based synthetic respondent could pass attention checks, produce a coherent demographic persona with memory of its past responses, and produce plausible survey based responses on a variety of prompts. This suggested that self-report survey results from online experiments may already be contaminated by bots.
Self-report surveys are only one type of behavioral data acquisition, with another key type being tasks, which often involve making speeded responses (~500-1000ms) often based upon arbitrary rules. Given LLMs and agentic AI tools often have slower latencies than those required in speeded tasks, such speeded tasks were thought to be less susceptible to contamination from agentic AI bots.
Recent work has suggested that even speeded tasks may be susceptible to agentic AI bots, but this existing work has key shortcomings. Husky et al. (2026) recently used a Claude Opus 4.6 (Anthropic) based bot to complete the Attention Network Task (Fan et al., 2006), a prominent speeded RT task, with performance that matched many patterns in human data. However, this solution was tailored to a single online task, required considerable training and iteration, and some key bot behaviors diverged from humans (e.g., long tails of the RT distribution). In a brief preprint and a set of demonstration videos, Ozudogru et al. (2026) showed that a prompt-only approach could produce human-like RT distributions and mean RTs in 3 different cognitive tasks. However, they provide minimal explanation of the bots architecture and minimal evaluation of the correspondence with human behavior.
Here, we present an agentic AI bot that addresses these shortcomings with minimal training and iteration, the ability to generalize across multiple tasks and multiple implementations of each task, and it produces specific behavioral patterns including trial-by-trial sequential effects.

**Methods:** Given only an experiment URL, the bot scrapes the page source and runtime JavaScript state and passes this to Claude Opus 4.8 (Anthropic) through a staged reasoning pipeline (structural analysis of the scraped source; behavioral parameterization; literature citation with programmatic DOI verification against OpenAlex; parameter-sensitivity tagging). Claude analyzes the source as a cognitive scientist would, identifying the task paradigm and generating a versioned TaskCard that specifies: response distributions (ex-Gaussian mu, sigma, and tau per condition), per-condition accuracy and omission rate targets, sequential RT effects (if applicable), and between-subject variance parameters — all grounded in Claude's knowledge of the behavioral literature and without any task-specific instruction from the researcher. Two aspects constitute the researcher's primary influence on behavior: the choice of distribution family (ex-Gaussian) and the provision of a menu of eight generic temporal RT mechanisms (autocorrelation, fatigue drift, condition repetition, 1/f noise, lag-1 pair modulation, post-event slowing, practice effect, vigilance decrement) from which Claude selects and parameterizes as appropriate for the identified task; paradigm-specific phenomena such as post-error or post-stop-signal slowing are expressed as configurations of the generic post-event-slowing mechanism rather than as named primitives, so the bot's library carries no paradigm vocabulary. A brief automated pilot session (~20 trials) validates Claude's stimulus detection selectors against the live experiment DOM, triggering a targeted refinement loop if selectors fail. The resulting TaskCard is cached (content-addressed by hash) and reused for all subsequent runs; no session in the evaluated cohorts required LLM calls at execution time (a bounded adaptive-navigation fallback — at most 10 LLM calls per session, for unfamiliar instruction flows — exists but fired only on one excluded partial session). We evaluated this pipeline on four implementations of two tasks — stop signal (ExpFactory RDoC, N=41 bot sessions; STOP-IT, N=41 bot sessions) and Stroop color-word (ExpFactory RDoC, N=45; Cognition.run, N=43) — comparing bot performance to human reference data from the RDoC battery (stop signal: N=2,412 sessions; Stroop: N=2,478 sessions), with bot cohort means z-positioned within the human between-session distribution (`experiment-bot-compare`).

**Results:** For the stop signal task, bot performance on the ExpFactory implementation fell within the human distribution on all key metrics: mean go RT (bot: 622 ± 47 ms; human: 649 ± 100 ms; z = −0.27), stop signal accuracy (bot: 53.2%; human: 52.1% ± 2.4%; z = +0.45), and estimated SSRT (bot: 214 ms; human: 234 ± 45 ms; z = −0.46). This pattern replicated across the STOP-IT implementation without modification to the configuration pipeline, demonstrating cross-platform generalization. For the Stroop task, the Stroop interference effect (incongruent − congruent RT) fell within the human range (bot: 93 ms; human: 67 ± 41 ms; z = +0.61) and congruent accuracy (95.1%) closely matched human norms (96.1% ± 4.7%). However, absolute RTs were systematically elevated relative to the human mean (congruent RT: bot: 706 ± 42 ms; human: 575 ± 67 ms; z = +1.95; incongruent RT: bot: 798 ± 56 ms; human: 642 ± 81 ms; z = +1.93), indicating that while the bot captures the qualitative structure of Stroop interference, absolute RT calibration for this task remains an area for improvement. Beyond mean-level metrics, the bot produced sequential RT dynamics consistent with human performance: positive lag-1 RT autocorrelation (stop signal: r = .10 ± .09; Stroop: r = .16 ± .10) and positive post-error slowing (stop signal: 37 ± 54 ms; Stroop: 60 ± 59 ms). Critically, and in contrast to Özüdoğru et al. (2026), these comparisons are made against a large human reference dataset using z-scores relative to the human distribution, not visual inspection of RT histograms.

**Conclusion:**  This work shows that current agentic AI tools, with minimal human iteration, can produce human-like performance across multiple tasks. This proof of concepts shows that real-world data acquisition in speeded cognitive tasks may be susceptible to bot contamination today.
